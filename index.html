  • IEEE Haptics

    Local Anesthesia Injection Simulation for clinical dentistry training

    Introduction

    This paper discusses the design and development of a custom haptic syringe aimed to improve a virtual reality local anesthesia simulation (VRLA) that formally used a head mounted device (HMD) with hand controllers for haptics. A pilot study investigated the effectiveness of the custom haptic syringe to replace one of the hand controllers to provide a more realistic and better training experience.

    Method

    A one group pre-post survey investigated student perceptions regarding the effectiveness of a haptic syringe to provide a more realistic experience to train dental students to learn the procedural steps to inject local anesthesia into a virtual patient. Twenty-two third year dental students participated. The pre-post survey examined changes in students’ perceived emotions, preparedness, and effectiveness of the VRLA as a foundational learning strategy prior to entering clinical group practice.

    Results

    While quantitative results on comparable survey questions did not yield statistically significant differences, qualitative open-ended responses revealed a much greater satisfaction using the custom haptic syringe compared to the out of the box controllers.

    Conclusions

    Feedback regarding the haptic syringe showed a substantial step closer to providing a more real experience for students in an effort to create a better bridge between classroom learning and clinical practice in order to prepare students to more confidently and competently work with real clients. Further refinement of the syringe is forthcoming along with a more formal comparative experimental study.

  • IEEE Haptics

    Haptic Equillibrium - The convergence of dominant and submissive force

    Abstract

    A collaborative virtual environment (CVE) with a haptic interaction provides a medium for users to interact using tactile sensation and feel each other physical forces. Extensive research has shown that virtual collaborative interactions with haptic feedback show better performance in a task than a virtual collaborative interaction with no-force feedback. Most of the research studies assume that the human agent is in a bidirectional human-to-human collaborative task at the same skill level. Humans have different skill levels and tactile responses to the same situations due to a combination of factors, including individual differences in sensory processing, motor control, cognitive abilities, experience, and learning Haptic interaction can only be useful in particular tasks. Most of the research studies use similar tasks like Cooperative Object Manipulation with similar levels of difficulty and complexity. This study aims to understand the haptic interaction between human agents in collaborative tasks of different complexity. The objective of this experiment is to also understand the force dynamics of human agents at different levels of task complexity. Finally, the aim is to understand the relationship between dominant force and submissive force in a collaborative haptic environment.

  • Adelaide, Australia

    Summer Internship in australia

    During my summer research, I worked at the intersection of virtual reality (VR) and artificial intelligence (AI) to enhance communication in remote collaborative environments. The primary focus of my research was to explore how large language models (LLMs) can be leveraged to detect and interpret gaze cues from speech, aiming to improve the effectiveness of communication in virtual reality settings.


    The objective of my research was to understand how natural conversation dynamics could be influenced by the integration of implicit gaze visualizations within a VR environment. By utilizing LLMs alongside shared gaze visualizations, we aimed to create more immersive interfaces that support seamless communication without relying on traditional methods such as detailed descriptive language, complex gestures, hand pointing, or sketching annotations. This approach holds the potential to significantly enhance collaboration by reducing the need for manual activation or rendering of communication cues, thereby streamlining the process of directing attention between remote users.


    I was responsible for developing the entire software pipeline, from fine-tuning the LLM to suit our specific task, to developing a networked application using the Unity game engine. Initially, I applied natural language processing (NLP) techniques, such as entity recognition systems, to detect deictic language in conversations. However, this approach did not fully capture the context of communication, resulting in suboptimal outcomes.


    To address this, I fine-tuned an LLM known as LLama-8B using parameter-efficient techniques like QLoRA. Due to the limited availability of datasets for detecting deictic language, the fine-tuned model was trained on a relatively small dataset and repurposed for binary classification. The model was trained to recognize phrases such as "Can you look at that?" or "Do you see that?" and subsequently activate visual cues to direct the attention of the other user.


    In addition, I developed a networked multiplayer application using Unity's Netcode, enabling users to create rooms, join sessions, and interact as virtual avatars with head and hand tracking. I integrated OpenAI's Whisper model to convert speech to text, allowing the LLM to process the conversation. Whisper's voice activity detection feature automatically paused transcription when no activity was detected, enhancing the system's efficiency.


    I chose the Oculus Quest Pro for its eye-tracking capabilities, enabling me to render a line from the user's eye to the object they were looking at. In the VR environment, if one user intended to direct another user's attention towards a specific object, the LLM would detect the deictic language used and activate the visual cue, effectively guiding the second user's focus.


    To improve the accuracy of LLM predictions, I implemented a sentence tokenizer, ensuring that only complete sentences were passed to the model for processing. This approach minimized errors that could arise from incomplete sentence analysis. Additionally, the LLM was deployed locally within the Unity game engine to eliminate latency issues associated with cloud-based processing.


    While the research is ongoing, with user studies yet to be conducted, our preliminary findings suggest that this integration of LLMs and gaze cues could facilitate more efficient and intuitive remote collaboration. By automating the transfer of communicative intent between users, we hope to enhance task efficiency and foster faster, more natural collaboration in mixed-reality environments.
