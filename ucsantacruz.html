    Local Anesthesia Injection Simulation for clinical dentistry training

    Introduction

    This paper discusses the design and development of a custom haptic syringe aimed to improve a virtual reality local anesthesia simulation (VRLA) that formally used a head mounted device (HMD) with hand controllers for haptics. A pilot study investigated the effectiveness of the custom haptic syringe to replace one of the hand controllers to provide a more realistic and better training experience.

    Method

    A one group pre-post survey investigated student perceptions regarding the effectiveness of a haptic syringe to provide a more realistic experience to train dental students to learn the procedural steps to inject local anesthesia into a virtual patient. Twenty-two third year dental students participated. The pre-post survey examined changes in students’ perceived emotions, preparedness, and effectiveness of the VRLA as a foundational learning strategy prior to entering clinical group practice.

    Results

    While quantitative results on comparable survey questions did not yield statistically significant differences, qualitative open-ended responses revealed a much greater satisfaction using the custom haptic syringe compared to the out of the box controllers.

    Conclusions

    Feedback regarding the haptic syringe showed a substantial step closer to providing a more real experience for students in an effort to create a better bridge between classroom learning and clinical practice in order to prepare students to more confidently and competently work with real clients. Further refinement of the syringe is forthcoming along with a more formal comparative experimental study.

  • IEEE Haptics

    Haptic Equillibrium - The convergence of dominant and submissive force

    Abstract

    A collaborative virtual environment (CVE) with a haptic interaction provides a medium for users to interact using tactile sensation and feel each other physical forces. Extensive research has shown that virtual collaborative interactions with haptic feedback show better performance in a task than a virtual collaborative interaction with no-force feedback. Most of the research studies assume that the human agent is in a bidirectional human-to-human collaborative task at the same skill level. Humans have different skill levels and tactile responses to the same situations due to a combination of factors, including individual differences in sensory processing, motor control, cognitive abilities, experience, and learning Haptic interaction can only be useful in particular tasks. Most of the research studies use similar tasks like Cooperative Object Manipulation with similar levels of difficulty and complexity. This study aims to understand the haptic interaction between human agents in collaborative tasks of different complexity. The objective of this experiment is to also understand the force dynamics of human agents at different levels of task complexity. Finally, the aim is to understand the relationship between dominant force and submissive force in a collaborative haptic environment.

    The university resolution platform aims to radically improve the way students and administrative departments at the university communicate and resolve student-related issues. Our vision is focused on developing a transparent, efficient, and easy-to-use web application that will function as a centralized point for tracking and resolving issues.

    1. Centralized Issue Tracking : This platform will serve as a one-stop solution for all issue-related activities. It will log and track various student issues, from academic concerns to administrative queries. This centralization ensures that no student query falls through the cracks and enables efficient monitoring.
    2. Transparent Process: Transparency is a core tenet of this platform. Students will be able to track the progress of their issues in real-time, understanding each step taken towards resolution. This clarity in process fosters trust and confidence in the university's administrative procedures.
    3. User-Friendly Interface: Ease of use is paramount. The platform will be designed with a user-friendly interface, making it accessible for all students regardless of their tech proficiency. Simplifying the process of issue reporting and tracking enhance student engagement with the system.
    4. Enhanced Accountability: By having a structured and transparent system, the platform will hold departments accountable for timely and effective issue resolution. This accountability is crucial for maintaining the integrity and efficiency of the process.

    A Custom Haptic Syringe to Improve a VR Local Anesthesia Simulation for Foundational Dental Education: A Feasibility Study

    Learn More

    Abstract Introduction: This paper presents the design, development, and usability evaluation of a custom haptic syringe aimed to improve the realism of a virtual reality local anesthesia simulation (VRLA), specifically for the inferior alveolar nerve block (IANB), that formally used a head mounted device (HMD) and vibration feedback via hand controllers. The custom haptic syringe interface provides a more realistic tactile sensation of plunging a real syringe in a human patient. This pilot study investigated the usability of the custom haptic syringe to replace one of the hand controllers to provide a more realistic and better training experience.
